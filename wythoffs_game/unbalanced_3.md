# Unbalanced 3-D Wythoff (1:3)

Ordinary 3-heap Nim, plus a coupled move that removes $k$ chips from one pile and $3k$ from another.

### Moves

- **Single-pile (Nim):** $[x-t,y,z],\ [x,y-t,z],\ [x,y,z-t]$.
- **Unbalanced ($1\!:\!3$ or $3\!:\!1$ ratio)**, for every pair of piles:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&[x-t,\ y-3t,\ z], && [x-3t,\ y-t,\ z], \\
&[x-t,\ y,\ z-3t], && [x-3t,\ y,\ z-t], \\
&[x,\ y-t,\ z-3t], && [x,\ y-3t,\ z-t].
\end{aligned}
$$

Unlike $1\!:\!1$ Wythoff, an $x$-coordinate can drop by $3t$, so an IN-position on the current sheet
can be generated by a P-position that **skips intermediate $x$-levels**. That is the one wrinkle the
recursion has to handle.

### Deriving the recursion operator $\mathcal{R}$ (inter-sheet)

**General rule.** An inter-sheet move that lowers $x$ by $a$ and a partner coordinate by $b$ (fixed
ratio $a\!:\!b$) becomes: *step back $a$ sheets, shift $b$ along the partner axis, and union in the
P-sheet from $a$ levels back.*

$$V_x \;=\; \mathcal{S}^{\,b}\big(V_{x-a} \cup P_{x-a}\big),$$

where $\mathcal{S}$ is the unit shift along the partner axis. The key point: the look-back depth is
$a$ (how far the move drops $x$), and the per-level shift is $b$. (Combined Wythoff is the special
case $a=b=1$.)

The four $x$-lowering moves instantiate this on the $y$ and $z$ axes:

| Move | $(a,b)$ | Operator |
|------|---------|----------|
| $[x-t,\ y-3t]$ | $(1,3)$ | $V^2_x = \mathcal{Y}^3\big(V^2_{x-1} \cup P_{x-1}\big)$ |
| $[x-3t,\ y-t]$ | $(3,1)$ | $V^3_x = \mathcal{Y}\big(V^3_{x-3} \cup P_{x-3}\big)$ |
| $[x-t,\ z-3t]$ | $(1,3)$ | $V^4_x = \mathcal{Z}^3\big(V^4_{x-1} \cup P_{x-1}\big)$ |
| $[x-3t,\ z-t]$ | $(3,1)$ | $V^5_x = \mathcal{Z}\big(V^5_{x-3} \cup P_{x-3}\big)$ |

Plus Nim X, $V^1_x = \bigcup_{x'<x} P_{x'}$. Then $W_x = V^1_x \cup V^2_x \cup V^3_x \cup V^4_x \cup
V^5_x$.

Note moves $V^3, V^5$ step back $a=3$ levels, so they reference $P_{x-3}$ **and** $V_{x-3}$ ‚Äî *not*
$V_{x-1}$. (Shifting $V_{x-1}$ by $1$ each level would wrongly create shadows on sheets that move 3
cannot actually reach, since it advances $x$ in steps of $3$.) This is why the recursion keeps a
**3-level-deep history**.

**In code:** `L_prev1/L_prev2/L_prev3` are $P_{x-1}, P_{x-2}, P_{x-3}$. The $(1,3)$ moves use
`Acc2_prev`/`Acc4_prev` ($V_{x-1}$) shifted by 3 (`[:, 3:] = temp[:, :-3]`). The $(3,1)$ moves use
`Acc3_m3`/`Acc5_m3` ‚Äî the accumulator from **3** levels back ($V_{x-3}$) ‚Äî unioned with `L_prev3`
($P_{x-3}$) and shifted by 1. `ever_lost` is the Nim-X union $V^1$. After each sheet the histories
roll forward one slot (`L_prev3 = L_prev2`, `Acc3_m3 = Acc3_m2`, ‚Ä¶).

### Supermex operator $\mathcal{M}$ (intra-sheet)

With $x$ fixed the in-sheet moves are Nim Y, Nim Z, and the two unbalanced $(y,z)$ moves
$[y-t, z-3t]$ and $[y-3t, z-t]$. Each new P-position therefore blocks four families of cells: its
full row (Nim Y), its full column (Nim Z), and the two unbalanced rays of slope $1\!:\!3$ and
$3\!:\!1$ rising from it.

**In code:** `supermex` scans the sheet; at each cell not yet blocked it marks a loser and casts the
four blocking rays ‚Äî `(y+t)`, `(z+t)`, `(y+t, z+3t)`, and `(y+3t, z+t)` ‚Äî into the `blocked` mask.
